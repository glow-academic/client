What we’re practicing: translating loops into summations, converting summations to closed forms, and comparing growth rates using $O(\cdot)$, $\Omega(\cdot)$, and $\Theta(\cdot)$.

Instructions

  • Assume Work() runs in constant time, with cost $c > 0$ per call, independent of input.
  • For “closed form,” give an exact expression (not just $O(\cdot)$) unless the prompt says otherwise.
  • Show your reasoning: summations are fine, but don’t stop at a raw double-sum.
  • Logarithms are base 2 unless stated otherwise (the base doesn’t matter for asymptotics).
Question 1 — Counting Nested Loop Work
15 pts
Let $T(n)$ be the total cost contributed by calls to Work() in the algorithm below. Derive a closed form for $T(n)$ (exact, in terms of $n$ and $c$), and give the tight $\Theta(\cdot)$ bound. 
function B1(n: positive integer):
  total ← 0
  for i from 1 to n by multiplying by 4 do
    for j from 1 to i do
      total ← total + Work()
    end for
  end for
  return total
end function
Hint: the outer loop visits $i = 1, 4, 16, \dots$ up to $n$. Convert to a geometric series of partial sums.
Question 2 — Return Value as a Closed Form
20 pts
The following function returns a number (not a runtime). Derive the exact value returned, as a function of $n$. You may assume $n$ is a positive integer. 
function B2(n: positive integer):
  s ← 0
  for i from 1 to n do
    for j from 1 to (2i - 1) do
      s ← s + 1
    end for
  end for
  return s
end function
Hint: recognize the inner loop length and use a known summation identity.
Question 3 — True/False on Asymptotic Claims
3 × 6 pts
For each statement, mark it True or False. Provide a short justification: either a proof sketch (e.g., limits, inequalities) or a counterexample.
  1. $n^3 = O(2^n)$
  2. $\dfrac{\log n}{\log\log n} = O(\sqrt{\log n})$
  3. $n\log n = \Omega(n^{1.1})$
Tip: if you use limits, explicitly state what you’re taking the limit of and why it implies the bound.
Question 4 — Ordering by Growth Rate + A Property Proof
25 pts
(a) Sort the following functions from slowest growth to fastest growth (increasing order). If two functions are $\Theta(\cdot)$ of each other, group them together and briefly justify.
$f_1(n)=n^{2.5},\quad f_2(n)=3^n,\quad f_3(n)=n^2\log n,\quad f_4(n)=2^{n/3}\cdot n^7,\quad f_5(n)=(\log n)^8$
(b) Suppose $f(n)$ and $g(n)$ are functions that are eventually positive (there exists $n_0$ such that for all $n\ge n_0$, $f(n)>0$ and $g(n)>0$). Prove:
$\min\{f(n), g(n)\} = \Theta\!\left(\dfrac{f(n)\,g(n)}{f(n)+g(n)}\right)$
(c) Give an explicit example of functions $f,g$ (eventually positive) such that neither $f=O(g)$ nor $g=O(f)$ holds. Briefly justify.
Tip for (b): bound the expression between constant multiples of $\min\{f,g\}$ using simple inequalities like $f+g \le 2\max\{f,g\}$ and $f+g \ge \max\{f,g\}$.
