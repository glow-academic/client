Probability and Hypothesis Testing Problems

Problem 1

A quality control engineer tests a batch of 100 electronic components. The probability that any single component is defective is 0.02, and components are independent.

a) What is the probability that exactly 3 components are defective?

b) What is the probability that at least 2 components are defective?

c) What is the expected number of defective components?

Solution:

This is a binomial distribution problem with n = 100 and p = 0.02.

a) P(X = 3) = C(100,3) × (0.02)³ × (0.98)⁹⁷

Using the binomial formula: P(X = 3) = 100!/(3!×97!) × (0.02)³ × (0.98)⁹⁷

Calculation yields approximately 0.1823

b) P(X ≥ 2) = 1 - P(X = 0) - P(X = 1)

P(X = 0) = (0.98)¹⁰⁰ ≈ 0.1326

P(X = 1) = 100 × 0.02 × (0.98)⁹⁹ ≈ 0.2707

P(X ≥ 2) = 1 - 0.1326 - 0.2707 = 0.5967

c) E[X] = np = 100 × 0.02 = 2 defective components

Problem 2

A researcher wants to test the hypothesis that the mean height of a population is 68 inches. They take a sample of 50 individuals and find a sample mean of 69.2 inches with a sample standard deviation of 3.5 inches.

a) State the null and alternative hypotheses.

b) Calculate the test statistic.

c) Determine the p-value for a two-tailed test at α = 0.05.

d) What is your conclusion?

Solution:

a) H₀: μ = 68 inches
H₁: μ ≠ 68 inches

b) Since n = 50 > 30, we can use the z-test:

z = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s/√n) = (69.2 - 68) / (3.5/√50)

z = 1.2 / (3.5/7.071) = 1.2 / 0.495 = 2.424

c) For a two-tailed test with z = 2.424:

P-value = 2 × P(Z > 2.424) = 2 × 0.0077 = 0.0154

d) Since p-value (0.0154) < α (0.05), we reject the null hypothesis. There is sufficient evidence to conclude that the mean height is different from 68 inches.
