Organic Chemistry Reaction Mechanisms

Problem 1

Provide a complete mechanism for the following reaction, showing all intermediates and electron flow:

CH₃CH₂Br + OH⁻ → CH₃CH₂OH + Br⁻

a) Is this an SN1 or SN2 reaction? Justify your answer.

b) What would happen to the rate if the concentration of OH⁻ is doubled?

c) What would happen if the leaving group is changed from Br⁻ to F⁻?

Solution:

Mechanism (SN2):

  1. Nucleophile (OH⁻) attacks the electrophilic carbon from the backside, simultaneously breaking the C-Br bond
  2. Transition state with pentacoordinate carbon (partial bonds to C, Br, and OH)
  3. Br⁻ leaves, forming the product alcohol

a) This is an SN2 reaction because:

  • The substrate is a primary alkyl halide (CH₃CH₂Br)
  • The reaction is bimolecular (rate depends on both [RX] and [Nu⁻])
  • The mechanism involves a single step with inversion of configuration
  • Strong nucleophile (OH⁻) and good leaving group (Br⁻)

b) Since this is SN2, the rate = k[CH₃CH₂Br][OH⁻]. If [OH⁻] is doubled, the rate will also double (linear relationship).

c) If Br⁻ is changed to F⁻, the rate would decrease significantly because:

  • F⁻ is a poor leaving group (strong C-F bond, high bond energy)
  • The rate of SN2 reactions is highly sensitive to leaving group ability
  • Br⁻ is a much better leaving group than F⁻ due to weaker C-Br bond and greater stability of Br⁻ ion
Problem 2

Predict the major product(s) for each of the following reactions and provide mechanisms:

a) (CH₃)₃CBr + CH₃OH →

b) CH₃CH=CH₂ + HBr →

c) Cyclohexene + Br₂ →

Solution:

a) (CH₃)₃CBr + CH₃OH → (CH₃)₃COCH₃ + HBr

Mechanism (SN1):

  1. Tertiary bromide undergoes heterolysis to form tert-butyl carbocation and Br⁻
  2. CH₃OH (weak nucleophile) attacks the carbocation to form (CH₃)₃COCH₃
  3. Proton transfer from protonated ether to Br⁻ gives final product

This is SN1 because tertiary alkyl halide favors carbocation formation.

b) CH₃CH=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃CHBrCH₃

Mechanism (Electrophilic Addition, Markovnikov):

  1. HBr protonates alkene to form secondary carbocation (CH₃CH⁺CH₃)
  2. Br⁻ attacks carbocation to form 2-bromopropane

Markovnikov's rule: H adds to carbon with more hydrogens.

c) Cyclohexene + Br₂ → trans-1,2-dibromocyclohexane

Mechanism (Electrophilic Addition, Anti addition):

  1. Br₂ attacks alkene to form bromonium ion intermediate
  2. Br⁻ attacks from opposite side (anti addition) to form trans-dibromide

The product is racemic trans-1,2-dibromocyclohexane.

Problem 3

For the following molecule, identify all chiral centers and determine the maximum number of stereoisomers possible:

HOOC-CH(OH)-CH₂-CH(NH₂)-COOH

Draw all stereoisomers and label each as enantiomer or diastereomer.

Solution:

Structure analysis: HOOC-CH(OH)-CH₂-CH(NH₂)-COOH

This is HOOC-CH(OH)-CH₂-CH(NH₂)-COOH

Chiral centers: C₂ (CH(OH)) and C₄ (CH(NH₂))

Maximum stereoisomers: 2ⁿ where n = number of chiral centers

Maximum = 2² = 4 stereoisomers

Stereoisomers:

  • (2R, 4R): Both chiral centers have R configuration
  • (2S, 4S): Both chiral centers have S configuration (enantiomer of 2R,4R)
  • (2R, 4S): Opposite configurations (diastereomer)
  • (2S, 4R): Opposite configurations (diastereomer, enantiomer of 2R,4S)

Relationships:

  • (2R,4R) and (2S,4S) are enantiomers
  • (2R,4S) and (2S,4R) are enantiomers
  • All other pairs are diastereomers

If the molecule has no internal plane of symmetry and no meso form, all 4 stereoisomers are distinct.

Problem 4

Explain why 1,3-butadiene undergoes 1,4-addition with HBr at elevated temperatures, while 1,2-addition is favored at lower temperatures. Provide energy diagrams and discuss the thermodynamic vs. kinetic control.

Solution:

Reaction: 1,3-butadiene + HBr

At low temperature (kinetic control):

  • 1,2-addition product (3-bromo-1-butene) forms faster
  • This is the kinetically favored product (lower activation energy)
  • The transition state leading to 1,2-addition is more stable (more substituted allylic carbocation)

At high temperature (thermodynamic control):

  • 1,4-addition product (1-bromo-2-butene) is more stable
  • Higher temperature allows equilibrium to be established
  • 1,4-product has more stable double bond (disubstituted vs. monosubstituted)
  • System reaches thermodynamic equilibrium

Energy Diagram Explanation:

  • 1,2-addition: Lower Eₐ (kinetic product), but higher ΔG (less stable)
  • 1,4-addition: Higher Eₐ (slower formation), but lower ΔG (more stable)
  • At low T: Reaction doesn't have enough energy to reverse, so kinetic product accumulates
  • At high T: Reaction can reverse, allowing system to reach thermodynamic minimum (1,4-product)

This is a classic example of kinetic vs. thermodynamic control in organic reactions.
